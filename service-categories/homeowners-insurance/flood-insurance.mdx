Flood insurance is a separate insurance policy that covers property damage caused by flooding. Standard homeowners insurance policies do not cover flood damage, making separate flood coverage necessary for properties in flood-prone areas.

Floods are the most common natural disaster in the United States. Even properties outside designated high-risk zones can experience flooding from heavy rain, storm surges, or drainage issues.

## Why Flood Insurance Is Separate

Homeowners insurance excludes flood damage because flooding affects entire regions simultaneously. When a flood occurs, all properties in the area file claims at once, creating concentrated risk that standard insurance models cannot absorb.

This exclusion applies universally. No standard homeowners policy covers flood damage, regardless of the insurance company or policy type.

## Who Needs Flood Insurance

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Federally Required">
    Mortgage lenders require flood insurance for properties in FEMA-designated Special Flood Hazard Areas (SFHAs). These high-risk zones have a 1% or greater annual chance of flooding, sometimes called "100-year flood zones."

    The requirement remains for the life of the mortgage. Lenders can force-place flood insurance if coverage lapses, typically at higher premiums.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Recommended but Not Required">
    Properties outside high-risk zones still experience floods. FEMA reports that over 40% of flood insurance claims come from properties in moderate-to-low risk areas.

    Factors that increase flood risk regardless of zone designation:

    - Proximity to streams, rivers, or coastlines
    - Low-lying areas or poor drainage
    - Properties downhill from developed areas
    - Regions with heavy rainfall patterns
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## Types of Flood Insurance

### National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP)

The NFIP is a federal program administered by FEMA. It provides standardized flood coverage through participating insurance companies.

**Coverage limits:**

- Building coverage: up to \$250,000 for residential properties
- Contents coverage: up to \$100,000

**Key characteristics:**

- Same rates and coverage regardless of which company sells the policy
- Available in communities that participate in NFIP floodplain management
- 30-day waiting period before coverage begins (with some exceptions)

### Private Flood Insurance

Private insurers offer flood policies outside the NFIP. These policies may provide different coverage options and limits.

**Potential advantages:**

- Higher coverage limits than NFIP maximums
- Additional living expenses coverage (not included in NFIP)
- Replacement cost coverage options
- Shorter or no waiting periods in some cases

**Considerations:**

- Premiums and terms vary by insurer
- Coverage definitions may differ from NFIP
- Lenders must accept the policy if required for mortgage compliance

## What Flood Insurance Covers

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Building Coverage">
    Building coverage protects the physical structure and its systems:

    - Foundation and walls
    - Electrical and plumbing systems
    - HVAC equipment
    - Built-in appliances
    - Permanently installed carpeting
    - Water heaters and fuel tanks
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Contents Coverage">
    Contents coverage protects personal belongings inside the structure:

    - Furniture and electronics
    - Clothing and personal items
    - Portable appliances
    - Curtains and portable air conditioners
    - Washers and dryers

    Contents coverage is purchased separately from building coverage. Renters can purchase contents-only policies.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="What Is Not Covered">
    Flood insurance typically excludes:

    - Damage from moisture, mildew, or mold that could have been prevented
    - Currency, precious metals, and valuable papers
    - Property outside the building (landscaping, fences, pools)
    - Living expenses during repairs (NFIP policies)
    - Vehicles
    - Finished basement improvements (limited coverage under NFIP)
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## Cost Factors

Flood insurance premiums vary based on multiple factors:

- **Flood zone designation:** High-risk zones have higher premiums
- **Elevation:** Properties elevated above base flood elevation cost less to insure
- **Building characteristics:** Foundation type, number of floors, age of construction
- **Coverage amounts:** Higher limits increase premiums
- **Deductible selection:** Higher deductibles reduce premiums

<Note>
  FEMA implemented Risk Rating 2.0 in 2021, changing how NFIP premiums are calculated. This methodology considers more flood risk variables, resulting in premium changes for many properties.
</Note>

## Purchasing Flood Insurance

### Through NFIP

1. Contact any licensed insurance agent who sells flood insurance
2. Provide property information and flood zone determination
3. Select building and contents coverage amounts
4. Choose deductible levels
5. Coverage begins 30 days after purchase (standard waiting period)

### Private Flood Insurance

1. Request quotes from insurers offering private flood coverage
2. Compare coverage terms, limits, and exclusions
3. Verify lender acceptance if mortgage requires flood insurance
4. Review waiting period terms

<Tip>
  An Elevation Certificate documents a building's elevation relative to estimated flood levels. This certificate can affect premium calculations and may reduce costs for elevated properties.
</Tip>

## Questions to Ask

When purchasing flood insurance, consider asking:

- What is my property's flood zone designation?
- Does the NFIP or private insurance offer better coverage for my situation?
- What is the waiting period before coverage begins?
- Are there elevation certificate discounts available?
- What basement coverage limitations apply?
- How do deductible options affect my premium?
